# Smith Center Startup Launchpad PRD

Version: 0.1  
Date: 2026-05-03  
Status: Draft for university review  
Working title: Startup Launchpad: A Four-Semester Innovation Mindset Program

## 1. Executive Summary

Startup Launchpad is a four-semester extracurricular program that helps students build a startup and innovation mindset through repeated cycles of discovery, validation, building, pitching, feedback, and iteration. Students work in small cross-functional teams toward a venture, product, service, creative platform, research commercialization path, or community innovation project.

The program is designed for flexible entry. The primary entry points are Fall Year 1 for students beginning the full pathway and Fall Year 2 for students with an existing idea, prototype, capstone, research project, or early traction. Rolling entry remains open for students who want to join teams as business, engineering, design, content, research, or operations contributors.

The program uses two recurring annual events as milestone checkpoints: a fall Ideathon and a spring Innovation Festival. Advanced teams use the spring Innovation Festival to qualify for Sunstone, the advanced venture competition. The Launchpad tool is used as a training and project-development system to help teams create sound projects, document evidence, and prepare increasingly demanding pitches.

## 2. Problem Statement

Many students are interested in innovation but do not know how to move from curiosity to a credible venture. They may lack a team, customer validation skills, technical capability, design support, storytelling practice, or confidence navigating entrepreneurship resources. Existing events can create excitement, but without a structured pathway students often lose momentum after a pitch night.

Startup Launchpad solves this by turning disconnected events into a progressive two-year experience with multiple entry points, visible milestones, team formation, skill-building, mentor feedback, and increasing standards of evidence.

## 3. Program Vision

Students should experience entrepreneurship as a learnable practice rather than a personality trait. By the end of the pathway, students should understand how to find meaningful problems, test assumptions, work across disciplines, build prototypes, gather evidence, tell a compelling story, and decide whether an idea deserves to become a venture.

## 4. Goals

- Create a repeatable extracurricular pathway from idea discovery to venture readiness.
- Support students who enter with no idea, a rough idea, a prototype, or only a useful skill.
- Build cross-functional teams across business, engineering, design, content, research, and community domains.
- Use Ideathon, Innovation Festival, and Sunstone as progressively demanding milestones.
- Make the Launchpad tool a central training and evidence-capture system.
- Produce ventures with product, pilots, users, revenue, letters of intent, partnerships, or other traction by Spring Year 2.
- Give the university a visible, measurable innovation pipeline.

## 5. Non-Goals

- The program is not a for-credit replacement for entrepreneurship coursework.
- The program does not require every student team to become a legally formed company.
- The program does not assume all successful outcomes are investor-backed startups.
- The program should not reward pitch polish over evidence, learning, or ethical execution.

## 6. Target Users

### First-Year Explorer
Curious student with no idea yet. Needs exposure, confidence, team formation, and early wins.

### Builder With Prototype
Student or team with a prototype, capstone, creative project, or research result. Needs customer validation, commercialization support, and pitch readiness.

### Business or Strategy Lead
Student who can help with customer discovery, market sizing, financial models, partnerships, and operations but may not have a product.

### Designer, Content, or Storytelling Lead
Student who can help teams with user experience, brand, demos, media, narrative, and campaign development.

### Advanced Venture Team
Team with validation, product progress, and early traction. Needs funding, mentor access, business plan support, and Sunstone preparation.

## 7. Core Experience Loop

The program should repeat a simple loop every semester:

1. Provocation: Students encounter challenge prompts, market needs, research opportunities, or community problems.
2. Team Formation: Students find collaborators with complementary skills.
3. Customer Evidence: Teams test assumptions through interviews, observation, surveys, pilots, and market research.
4. Build/Test: Teams create prototypes, landing pages, service pilots, demos, content experiments, or technical proofs.
5. Pitch: Teams present to judges in a format appropriate to their stage.
6. Feedback: Judges, mentors, peers, and users identify the next highest-risk assumption.
7. Next Sprint: Teams return to the Launchpad tool and workshops to improve the project.

## 8. Four-Semester Program Roadmap

| Stage | Semester | Theme | Student Mission | Main Deliverable | Event Pitch |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Level 1 | Fall Year 1 | Discover | Find a meaningful problem and form a team | Problem brief, user segment, rough solution thesis | Ideathon: 90-second idea pitch |
| Level 2 | Spring Year 1 | Validate | Prove that someone has the problem and wants a better solution | Customer interviews, early prototype, Launchpad evidence record | Innovation Festival: 3-minute validation pitch |
| Level 3 | Fall Year 2 | Build | Turn validation into a venture-shaped MVP or pilot | MVP/pilot, team roles, business model, traction target | Advanced Ideathon: 5-minute progress pitch |
| Level 4 | Spring Year 2 | Launch | Demonstrate readiness for Sunstone and external support | Traction, business plan, go-to-market plan, financial logic | Innovation Festival: 7-10 minute Sunstone qualifier |

## 9. Entry and Placement Model

Students may enter through four tracks:

- Explorer Track: For students with no idea yet, typically Fall Year 1.
- Joiner Track: For students who want to join a team and contribute a skill, typically Spring Year 1 or any rolling entry point.
- Accelerator Track: For students with an idea, prototype, research project, capstone, or creative product, typically Fall Year 2.
- Contributor Track: For students with useful capabilities in design, engineering, business, storytelling, content, research, community engagement, or operations.

New students complete an intake that captures interests, skills, availability, preferred problem areas, and whether they want to found, join, advise, or explore. The program then recommends a track, team opportunities, workshops, and event targets.

## 10. Event Ladder

| Event | Purpose | Pitch Standard | Judging Emphasis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fall Ideathon, Year 1 | Spark ideas and teams | 90-second problem and idea pitch | Problem clarity, urgency, originality, team potential |
| Spring Innovation Festival, Year 1 | Validate early concepts | 3-minute validation pitch | Customer evidence, prototype learning, next assumptions |
| Fall Ideathon, Year 2 | Accelerate advanced teams | 5-minute progress pitch | MVP progress, traction target, team capability, feasibility |
| Spring Innovation Festival, Year 2 | Select Sunstone-ready ventures | 7-10 minute venture pitch with Q&A | Desirability, feasibility, viability, traction, readiness |
| Sunstone | Advanced venture competition | Business plan and investor-style pitch | Launch readiness, evidence, market, execution, impact |

## 11. Launchpad Tool Role

The Launchpad tool is a required training and project-development layer. It should help students:

- Define the problem, customer, and opportunity.
- Track hypotheses, assumptions, interviews, experiments, and evidence.
- Maintain a living project brief.
- Identify missing team skills.
- Prepare pitch materials for each event.
- Convert judge feedback into the next sprint plan.

Recommended Launchpad modules:

- Idea and problem framing.
- Customer discovery.
- Prototype planning.
- Business model assumptions.
- Team role mapping.
- Pitch builder.
- Traction dashboard.
- Sunstone readiness checklist.

## 12. Challenge and Provocation Strategy

The program should use external challenge models as idea generators and aspiration benchmarks:

- Stanford Center on Longevity Design Challenge: human-centered, theme-based design prompts.
- NASA Space Apps Challenge: interdisciplinary sprint model using science, data, design, and storytelling.
- Hult Prize: social impact and global problem framing.
- VentureWell E-Team: invention, commercialization, customer discovery, and non-dilutive support.
- MIT $100K: staged competition model from pitch to accelerate to launch.
- Rice Business Plan Competition: advanced venture and investor-readiness benchmark.
- Sunstone Innovation Challenge / CSU Startup Launch model: advanced business plan, mentorship, seed funding, and venture-readiness framework.

## 15. Success Metrics

### Participation
- Number of students enrolled by semester.
- Number of new students entering through each track.
- Retention from event to event.
- Number of students joining teams after rolling entry.

### Team Formation
- Number of cross-disciplinary teams.
- Number of teams with business, technical, design, and storytelling coverage.
- Number of mentor matches.

### Venture Progress
- Customer interviews completed.
- Prototypes, pilots, demos, or MVPs created.
- Teams with users, revenue, letters of intent, partnerships, waitlists, or usage data.
- Teams qualifying for Innovation Festival advanced review.
- Teams applying to or qualifying for Sunstone.

### University Value
- Student learning outcomes.
- Alumni and community engagement.
- Sponsor participation.
- External competition participation.
- Startups, social ventures, creative ventures, patents, pilots, internships, or community projects launched.

## 16. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
| --- | --- |
| Students drop after one event | Use recurring sprints, mentor follow-ups, and visible next milestones |
| Teams lack key skills | Run contributor track, team marketplace, and skill-specific workshops |
| Pitch polish outruns evidence | Weight judging rubrics toward validation, learning, and traction |
| Late joiners feel behind | Provide onboarding modules, placement tracks, and team-join pathways |
| Too many weak ideas advance | Use stage gates based on evidence, not enthusiasm alone |
| Faculty/staff capacity is too thin | Create mentor pods, student ambassadors, reusable templates, and office-hour blocks |
